42 System Information

Overview of 42 School and Its Methodology

42 is a global network of free coding schools, with locations around the world. The school is famous for its unconventional approach to education, especially in the field of computer science. It offers no teachers, no traditional classes, and no tuition fees. Instead, it uses a peer-to-peer learning model where students learn by working on real-life projects.

The core focus of 42 is not just technical proficiency, but also developing problem-solving skills, resilience, and teamwork. The school encourages students to learn at their own pace, with no deadlines or exams, and the entire process is driven by motivation and the ability to self-teach. 42 is designed to create a strong technical workforce that can adapt to the constantly evolving tech industry.

The Piscine: The Selection Process

The first step into 42 is the Piscine (French for “swimming pool”), a rigorous and intensive month-long coding bootcamp that acts as a selection process for admission into the full program.

Duration and Intensity: The Piscine lasts about 4 weeks (roughly 26 days) and is a highly demanding period where prospective students must complete a series of programming challenges. It’s designed to test their ability to work under pressure, to solve problems creatively, and to collaborate with peers. It’s a grind, with long hours (usually 10-12 hours per day), and candidates are expected to be dedicated and fully immersed in the experience.

Focus Areas:

  • C Programming: The Piscine mainly focuses on learning C, a foundational programming language. Students will solve a wide array of problems that develop their understanding of algorithms, data structures, and logic.
  • Problem Solving: It emphasizes logical thinking, algorithmic problem-solving, and debugging.
  • Peer Review: Instead of traditional teachers, feedback comes from peers. This method helps students learn how to work collaboratively, fix mistakes, and grow together.
  • Collaboration: The Piscine is designed for applicants to help each other solve problems. No single person is expected to complete every task alone. Instead, the focus is on teamwork, and helping your peers is just as important as solving your own problems.

Result: At the end of the Piscine, those who perform well (in terms of problem-solving ability, perseverance, and teamwork) are offered a spot in the 42 Common Core program. The evaluation process is based on performance and attitude, not just raw programming knowledge.

The Check-In Process

Before the Piscine, applicants need to complete the Check-In process, which serves as an introduction to the 42 school philosophy.

Application: First, you'll need to submit your application online, which includes filling out a questionnaire to show your interest in programming and problem-solving.

Initial Test: Some 42 campuses have an online check-in exam or test, designed to evaluate basic logic and problem-solving skills. This may not be a requirement at all campuses but is used in some locations to ensure that applicants are ready for the intensity of the Piscine.

Preparation: It's strongly advised to prepare for the Piscine by getting comfortable with basic programming concepts like algorithms, loops, arrays, and pointers (especially in C). While prior knowledge is not mandatory, having a basic grasp will be helpful.

The Common Core: After the Piscine

Once you've completed the Piscine, assuming you're accepted into the program, you'll enter the Common Core phase.

Curriculum: The Common Core is the foundational stage of 42’s curriculum. It includes a series of projects that teach you key concepts of computer science, from low-level programming languages to algorithms, operating systems, and network protocols.

Peer Learning: Much like the Piscine, students continue to learn through peer interactions. You'll work on projects with fellow students, receive feedback, and mentor others in return. This continuous exchange of knowledge builds a sense of community and mutual growth.

Gamified Learning: The curriculum is gamified — students complete projects, and as you finish them, you earn points and levels. The progression system encourages constant learning, and you "level up" as you acquire new skills. This also means there’s no rigid schedule or deadlines — you learn at your own pace.

Exams: There are no traditional exams in the Common Core. Progression is determined entirely by project completion. If you successfully finish a project, you move on to the next. If you don’t meet the standards, you continue working on the same project until it’s complete.

End Goal: The Common Core is meant to equip students with all the basic skills needed for a career in tech. It covers topics such as algorithms, databases, operating systems, networking, and security, preparing you for specialized programming jobs.

How to Get a Spot Quickly: Tips for Success

Here are some tips to increase your chances of being accepted and successfully navigating the Piscine:

  • Start Practicing: Before the Piscine, try getting comfortable with programming basics like C, algorithms, and data structures. Websites like Codecademy, HackerRank, and freeCodeCamp are great resources for this.
  • Build Resilience: The Piscine is all about endurance. You'll be facing tough coding challenges, so cultivating a mindset of perseverance is key. There will be moments of frustration, but pushing through is part of the experience.
  • Collaborate: Since the program emphasizes collaboration, focus on improving your teamwork skills. Practice helping others with coding problems and discussing solutions with peers.
  • Stay Organized: Be sure to plan your schedule during the Piscine. The more organized you are with managing time, the better you’ll perform.
  • Stay Healthy: Make sure you get enough sleep, eat properly, and take breaks. While it’s easy to get consumed by the challenge, taking care of yourself is critical to maintaining energy and focus.

The Fine of the Common Core

After completing the Common Core, if you're still committed and progressing, you have the potential to enter into the more advanced stages of 42’s program, where you can specialize in certain areas like artificial intelligence, cybersecurity, game development, and more. The fine here is that, while there are no fees, students need to be self-driven and fully committed to this unique and rigorous self-paced journey.
